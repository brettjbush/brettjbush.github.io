1. Learn Git, love Git

...or SVN. No judging, we're all good! Either way, I can nearly guarantee you will be using some sort of version control system in your semicolon riddled adventures.

Git is one of the most widely used version control systems for code. Learn more here. It is a handy tool for keeping code organized as a single developer and absolutely invaluable for collaborating on code with others.

{% include mid_post_image.html image="octocat_01.jpg" caption="Octocat, Github's mascot, is also the best." alt="Octocat. Being awesome." %}

For instance, I didn't know Git was a thing until my last semester of college. How did I do group projects? Get ready: we would send code back and forth via... ZIP *shudder*.

With Git, anyone can collaborate on any code at any time and all the code stays nice and organized! You'll learn to love it, I promise.

2. Tests aren't over after you graduate

Scared you! Unit, Regression, and QA Tests, I mean. 😉  In order for code to be "air tight" it should be tested somehow.

Testing can get a bad rep. Whether you do it manually or (more recently, and what I've been working with) automated testing.

Just as you are about to slam dunk the coolest new feature into your team's code repo, you (or a QA specialist) have basically programmed a Shaq robot to stuff your precious code back in your face with a big red bruise that says "FAILED".

However, testing is actually really cool!

It took me a while to realize that a developer, on her or his own, can wear many hats at once: developer and tester. Not only can you write your own code, but you can write code to TEST your own code too!?!

Feels like your code it coming to life!

There are also a ton of resources out there to help test your code, so you're not alone. The more you think about testing, the more cool testing gets, and the more air tight and impressive your code is.

3. My code vs the code

This may be more personal opinion, but in my first year I lost something that had been weighing me down. Two words: "my code". What I got in return? "THE code". And I will tell you: THE code is WAYYY better then any of my code.

Let me explain.

There can be an artistry involved with programming. As an artist of the digital world, there's a natural amount of pride and satisfaction with writing your own stuff.

Feeling ownership over your code is totally natural and fine in itself, but my advice would be to lighten the reigns, just a little, especially in a team setting!

Why? A couple reasons I've found:

  • You feel more team oriented
  • You get the job done faster and often better by not reinventing wheels
  • You stop worrying about yourself being impressive and more the application being impressive
  • You become more comfortable with others giving advice, changes, or criticisms when you are more objective
  • Most importantly, if something goes wrong you don't have to feel salty since it's a team effort!

Now, when referring to code I've written, I take the emphasis off me with a simple change by calling it "the code" instead of "my code".

Don't get me wrong! You can do a lot of incredible things on your own, and you have all the right in the world to be proud about the accomplishments and apps you've made, but it's more the mentality of it all, and it's personally worked for me.

Be humble, be a team member, and stand on the shoulders of giants.

4. Learn to learn

Welcome to the tech industry! Where Moore's Law is always cranking out something new and better than before!

Within my first week I realized that there will always be more to learn, and that's not bad! In my first year I've been exposed to SO many languages, frameworks, and tools and am already preparing for more in the coming year.

Fortunately, there really is a lot you use from from school on a daily basis, and many of the things you need to learn are either related to or based off any initial tool set you come pre installed with.

It's actually one of the most fun parts of working! There is nothing static or bland about the day to day since there is always a new thing to learn and apply.

5. Quests

Quest Unlocked!

Or…issue unlocked! And assigned to you! Basically the same thing.

In college there was always a syllabus or instructive paper of what to do for a particular assignment. If you're working on a group project, you'd likely delegate tasks via word of mouth or on some document.

However, when you've got a larger amount of people working on the same product, it could be difficult to simply tell everyone what to do and avoid confusion.

Enter: Issue Tracking Systems

Once again, did not know these existed until my first year working, although I wish I did.

Why? Issue tracking systems like JIRA and Github's native one give you a measurable and definite goal to complete.

They only take a little bit of a learning curve to understand too, which is why I would totally recommend anyone to start using one sometime before their first year.

If you learn it with a friend too, you two can start making some cool apps in a organized and collaborative way.

Who doesn't like that!?

6. Organize your thoughts (and code snippets)

Keep a text editor (like Notepad++ or Atom) handy to keep your thoughts and code snippets in check.

I started one from day one once I realized it's a great tool for keeping organized and is handy as a temporary workspace, daily log, and dump space for commands or copies that you don't want to retype.

You will never regret having one up in the background.

7. The Internet is your friend, especially StackOverflow

As I like to joke: I refer to StackOverflow for a living.

While that's not entirely true, it is a huge help for day to day things!

Keep a search engine handy for everything from forgetting a command or running into a strange IDE error.

As awesome as it would be to know everything about every programming language and every error, unless you're Paul Bettany's character from the Marvel Cinematic Universe, that would be a tad difficult.

Fortunately, software developers and geek types with different areas of expertise come together on StackOverflow to answer your questions for you.

So watch that extra episode of Park and Rec! Someone already solved your bug a couple years ago on StackOverflow! All you have to do is seek the solution out online.

8. Agile vs Waterfall

There are two main workflow methodologies that I was introduced to in my first year: Agile and Waterfall.

Waterfall is basically where development goes in long strides across perhaps an entire half of a year. For example, the 2.0 release of some app would start in June and release that December.

However, current trends appear to be giving way to a new and more lively workflow methodology called Agile.

Agile is comprised of "sprints", 2-4 week hurdles that require some sort of demo-able and working prototype of some sort at the end of every sprint. Along with this, teams stay tight knit and often discuss what they're working on with each other, so everyone is in the know and working towards the same goal.

Check out Spotify on their practice of Agile!

9. Have fun and pursue your passion

This goes without saying, but it’s a reality that you can occasionally lose steam when caught up in the same thing.

Stay sharp and learn new things outside of work. This is how innovation and acceleration in your company and career happens. Incorporate what you love into your workday and work. Get excited and keep pursuing what you love!

All in all, it has been an amazing year of discovery and I'm excited for the year ahead to learn more.


I hope this list has helped anyone about to enter, actually entering, or just plain interested in the tech industry! Thanks for reading!
